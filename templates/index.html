Adventure

"Bikepacking": The Ultimate Freedom

Cycling on its own is a fantastic hobby, and I started learning about the depth of the sport in the summer of 2016. At the time, I was riding a hardtail mountain bike (suspension in the front) that allowed me to ride on the road and trails. I quickly found that I enjoyed a specific combination of quick and consistent speeds and trail riding. Likely because, as with most of my endeavors, I find satisfaction in pushing my mental and physical limits.

Enter the Great Allegheny Passage, a 150-mile trail stretching from Pittsburgh to Cumberland. I could not pass up the challenge, and in September of 2016 had an incredible adventure riding the GAP for three days. Carrying camping gear on my bike added an element that reminded me of the childhood enjoyment I found camping and connected the entire experience to nature in a way that felt like nirvana.

I had room to grow. The Chesapeake and Ohio Canal Towpath continues to Washington, Dc, another 185 miles from where the GAP leaves off. I was exhausted after the first trip, but I never waivered in the excitement that I would complete the entire trail system one day. In September 2018, I flew to Pittsburgh with a new cyclocross bicycle (pictured above). This time around, my dad accompanied me on the trail.

Day one statistics: 60 miles, 3064 feet of elevation, and 5 hours, 11 minutes in the saddle.

I remember passing the first campground from my 2016 trip and reflecting on my improvement in fitness. By the end of day one, all my dad and I could express was, "what did we get ourselves into." The weather was perfect, the trail was quiet and peaceful, and my legs felt strong.

Day two statistics: 90 miles, 7182 feet of elevation, and 8 hours, 18 minutes in the saddle.

I watched two and a half days pass before my eyes in one day. This stretch was my favorite section of the trail with all the biomes we passed through. It was steep and beautiful, which led to moments of complaint from our legs and mouthes but always followed by happiness about our situation. The last portion of the trail had a much-appreciated downhill section with one comically short uphill part. We hit that section in top gear. My mind was calm, my legs were sore, and that bump in the trail will forever be our enemy.

Day three statistics: 60 miles, 2395 feet of elevation, and 5 hours, 45 minutes in the saddle.

The C&O towpath was covered in amazing historical sites and things to stop at along the way. The towns were sparse and we ran out of water a few times. The temperature was up to 95F and punished us on the trail all day. This was a head-down day that we dug deep to complete.

Day four statistics: 66 miles, 2054 feet of elevation, and 6 hours, 32 minutes in the saddle.

I recall my knee hurt a lot this day. Peaceful and quiet section of the trail with more tree coverage than the day before. We stopped early for lunch but had to go off-trail and up a hill for a few miles. Riding next to a river was more noticeable and calming on this day. There is this excellent section where you ride on a concrete path cliffside of the river, gorgeous. The evening was enjoyable but I felt a little sad about the trip almost ending.

Day five statistics: 62.7 miles, 1434 feet of elevation, and 7 hours, 22 minutes in the saddle.

Rain. After days of clear skies, we spent the entire final day in the rain. I hit a breaking point that I was not expecting. 30 miles left and for the first time, I wanted the trip to be over. We sat in a small pizza restaurant and the rain finally lightened. I found enjoyment in the rest of the day and when we crossed the final mile marker in DC I was full of emotion. We rented a car and drove four hours back to the start of the trail. I do not think we stopped laughing during the entire car ride back. I will cherish my memories from this trip forever.
